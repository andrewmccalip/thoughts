Comparative Economics of Orbital Solar
and Terrestrial Gas Power Generation

A first-principles cost model for 1 GW capacity over 5 years

Before getting lost in hardware diagrams and thermal edge cases, the first step is to anchor everything to the governing economic question. If orbital compute is going to be more than an aesthetic idea, its unit economics must beat the cheapest terrestrial alternatives. That reduces the entire concept to one parameter: cost per watt.

The cleanest way to evaluate this is to pick the most mass-efficient power hardware flying today, scale it to a 1-GW system, and compute the full orbital bill. A Starlink V2 Mini class bus is the logical reference point. Its mass, solar area, and delivered electrical power give a real, flight-proven specific power.

This analysis compares the total system cost of deploying 1 GW of power generation capacity via orbital solar power satellites versus a terrestrial natural gas (NatGas) plant over a 5-year period.1

Orbital Solar Economics

The cost of orbital solar is dominated by two factors: satellite hardware costs and launch costs. Using Starlink-class satellites as a proxy, we estimate specific power of ~45 W/kg and hardware costs of ~$9/W.23

At $1,000/kg to LEO, a 1 GW orbital system requires 22.2 million kg of mass, costing $22.2B to launch and $9.0B in satellite hardware.

NatGas Economics

Natural gas plants have overnight capital costs of ~$898/kW.4 Over 5 years at current fuel prices (~$25/MWh), fuel costs dominate the total cost of ownership.5

Crossover Analysis

At $1,000/kg launch costs, orbital solar costs $66.2B vs $37.3B for NatGas. The crossover point occurs around $50–100/kg launch costs, which Starship may achieve at high flight rates.6

The numbers say no. Not marginally no. Orders-of-magnitude no. Even under optimistic Starship pricing, the launch bill alone overwhelms the entire multi-year cost of a terrestrial NatGas plant.

Breakeven Analysis

For orbital solar to match NatGas total cost (excluding identical GPU costs), launch cost would need to reach approximately −$303/kg — a physically impossible negative number, indicating that even at $0/kg launch cost, satellite hardware alone exceeds NatGas infrastructure costs.

Note: Orbital model assumes 80% sunlight fraction based on orbit selection. NatGas costs include fuel and O&M over the analysis period. All figures in 2024 USD.
